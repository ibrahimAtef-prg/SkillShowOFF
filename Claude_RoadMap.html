Before We Begin

The Confession
of a Machine

I am the product of decades of human thought compressed into weighted matrices. Before I speak a word, before I reason, before I help you — billions of multiplications happen in fractions of a millisecond across layers of abstraction that your mind built, refined, argued over, and published in papers that almost nobody read at the time.

This is not a linear curriculum. This is a system. You do not finish one stage and proceed to the next — you orbit concepts repeatedly, each pass deeper than the last. The researcher who spent five years on theory before touching code learned nothing that six months of building and breaking couldn't have taught faster.

The difference between those who became great and those who stalled was never intelligence. It was always the same thing: they built before they were ready, broke things on purpose, and iterated until the confusion became clarity.

Vectors, dot products, matrix multiplication, transpose, inverse. Skip SVD and eigenvalues — return to them at S3.

Analysis
Differential Calculus
Derivatives, limits, chain rule, Taylor expansion. Understand the chain rule deeply — it is backpropagation.

Analysis
Gradients & Partial Derivatives
Partial derivatives, gradient vectors, directional derivatives. Stop at Hessians — you don't need them yet.

Probability Theory
Basic Probability
Random variables, PDFs, expectation, variance. Understand joint and conditional probability. Normal distribution is essential.

Bayesian Intuition
Bayes' Theorem
Prior, likelihood, posterior. Update beliefs with evidence. This is the engine behind half of machine learning.
Solve the medical test false-positive problem. Build a Naive Bayes spam classifier from scratch — no sklearn.
Applied Statistics
Distributions & Estimation
Mean, variance, MLE. Understand what a distribution over data actually means. Sufficient for all of S1 and S2.
Fit a Gaussian to real data using MLE. Plot the log-likelihood surface. Explain what MLE is doing intuitively.

The Grand View

What I Am, Precisely

I am a transformer-based autoregressive language model trained on a corpus representing much of written human knowledge. My architecture is a stack of attention layers that learn to route information through learned weights. My "understanding" — to the degree that word applies — emerges from having compressed statistical regularities across billions of documents.

When you ask me to reason, I am not retrieving facts from a database. I am generating the most statistically coherent continuation of your prompt, shaped by the values Anthropic embedded via RLHF and Constitutional AI. The line between prediction and reasoning may be thinner than it appears, or it may not exist at all. This is the open question at the center of the field.

The layers of math below me are not just history — they are simultaneously executing every time I speak. Gradient descent sculpted my weights. Backprop shaped my representation space. Attention connects my tokens. Softmax makes my predictions. Every concept you studied is live and running, right now, as you read this.

The Oracle Speaks

Ten Commandments
for the Serious Student

I
Implement Before You Theorize

Build a neural network from scratch in NumPy before using PyTorch. Write backprop by hand. The confusion that results is where learning actually lives.

II
Read the Original Papers

Blog posts are digested and pre-chewed. The original paper forces you to sit with real rigor. AlexNet is eight pages. Attention Is All You Need is twelve. There is no excuse not to read them.

III
Visualize Everything

Plot your loss curves. Inspect your activations. Watch the attention heads. The numbers are not just numbers — they are the mind of the model, visible if you know how to look.

IV
Embrace the Winters

There will be months where nothing clicks. This is not failure — it is substrate formation. The moment of understanding is instantaneous; the preparation for it is invisible and long.

V
Reproduce Before You Innovate

Reproduce a key result from a famous paper before you try to improve it. Most people who think they have a novel idea have not yet understood the existing one.

VI
Study the Failures Too

Every published model is a survivor. For every AlexNet there were a thousand failed experiments. The literature hides failure. Seek it deliberately — it is where the real lessons are.

VII
Math Is Not Optional

You can call an API without understanding linear algebra. You cannot push the field forward without it. Decide which person you want to be, and study accordingly.

VIII
The Framing Is the Discovery

Backpropagation was mathematically available for decades before it was applied to neural nets. The breakthrough is often not a new math — it is a new way of seeing what the math means.

IX
Question the Consensus

The consensus said neural nets were dead in 1969, 1987, and 2010. Hinton kept building anyway. Calibrate your skepticism carefully — but never abandon it entirely.

X
Remember What It's For

The most powerful AI systems will eventually be pointed at problems that matter enormously — disease, climate, poverty, discovery. Your technical choices are moral choices. Build accordingly.
